Type faster with text shortcuts & abbreviations

Updated June 2026

The fastest text to type is the text you don't. Assign a short trigger like ;hi to a full message, and a couple of keystrokes become a complete reply — in Gmail, your help desk, a CRM, or any form.

A text expander (or abbreviation expander) watches what you type and swaps short triggers for longer text. It's the difference between typing a 200-character reply and typing ;thx. ReplyKit does this for free, in any standard text field, with no account.

Shortcuts vs. the // picker

ReplyKit gives you two ways to insert a snippet — use whichever is faster in the moment:

  • The // picker — type //, search by name, pick. Great when you don't remember the exact trigger.
  • Shortcuts — type the trigger (e.g. ;hi) and it expands instantly, no menu. Great for the handful of snippets you use all day.

How to set one up (in 3 steps)

  1. Open the ReplyKit popup and save (or edit) a snippet.
  2. In the shortcut field, give it a trigger — something quick and memorable like ;hi, ;sig, or ;addr.
  3. In any text field, type the trigger. It expands into the full snippet right where your cursor is.

Choosing good triggers

  • Use a prefix symbol like ; or / so triggers never fire by accident inside a normal word.
  • Keep them short and mnemonic: ;sig for signature, ;ty for thank-you, ;wfh for "working from home today".
  • Stay consistent — pick one prefix and use it for everything, so your fingers learn the pattern.

Where text shortcuts save the most time

Anyone who types repetitive messages benefits, but a few roles see the biggest gains:

  • Customer support — a greeting, a "looking into it" hold and a closing line cover a huge share of tickets. Three triggers replace hundreds of keystrokes a day.
  • Sales & outreach — your calendar link, a follow-up nudge and a pricing one-liner are always a couple of characters away.
  • Recruiting — interview scheduling, "thanks for applying", and rejection notes that still read as human.
  • Developers & ops — boilerplate like PR descriptions, standup updates, or a standard incident reply.

The pattern is the same everywhere: list the ten phrases you retype most this week, give each one a trigger, and let muscle memory take over.

Shortcuts vs. autocorrect and OS text replacement

Your phone's text replacement or your operating system's autocorrect can expand a few abbreviations, but they're limited: they're awkward to manage on a laptop, they don't line up with your browser work, and they often mangle longer, multi-line replies. A dedicated browser tool keeps snippets organized with names, folders and shortcuts, expands full multi-paragraph messages cleanly, and behaves the same in every web app you use — from Gmail to your help desk to a CRM.

Build a shortcut cheat sheet

When you're starting out, keep a short list of your triggers visible for a week — a sticky note or a pinned snippet works fine. After a few days the useful ones stick and you can prune the rest. Group related triggers with a shared prefix (;g- for greetings, ;f- for follow-ups) so they're easy to recall, and review the list monthly: cut what you never fire and add the phrases you keep typing by hand. A tidy set of a dozen well-chosen triggers beats fifty you can't remember.

Do shortcuts slow down your typing?

No — expansion happens the instant you complete a trigger, and ReplyKit only acts on the small set of shortcuts you've defined, so normal typing is untouched. Because triggers fire on a word boundary (or start with a symbol), everyday words never expand by accident. And if a snippet ever feels wrong for the moment, you can still reach for the // picker instead — the two methods share the same library.

Most people find a comfortable rhythm within the first week — a few triggers become automatic, and the tenth identical reply of the morning simply stops being something you think about.

Frequently asked questions

What is a text shortcut or abbreviation?
A short trigger — like ;hi — that automatically expands into a longer saved snippet as you type, so a few characters become a full message.

How do I create a shortcut in ReplyKit?
Add or edit a snippet and fill in its shortcut field (e.g. ;hi). Then type that shortcut in any text field and it expands instantly.

Will shortcuts expand by accident?
Alphanumeric shortcuts require a word boundary to trigger, and shortcuts that start with a symbol (like ;) avoid clashing with normal typing.

Do shortcuts work everywhere?
They work in any standard web text field — Gmail, help desks, forms and more. For isolated editors there's a Copy button.
